Golden Hour

There is a window each day that lasts no more than forty minutes. The light turns thick and amber, shadows stretch long across the ground, and everything ordinary becomes something worth photographing. Photographers call it golden hour, but it has never felt like an hour to me. It feels like a held breath — brief and impossible to keep.

I started chasing it years ago on a trip through the South Island of New Zealand. The mountains there catch the last light in a way that makes the rock look almost soft, like it might give under your hand. I remember pulling over on a gravel road outside Wanaka, no plan, no composition in mind, just reacting to the colour of the sky. That image is still one of my favourites — not because of technique, but because of the feeling I can still pull from it when I look at it.

These days I try not to overthink it. The best golden hour shots I have taken came from being in the right place and staying a little longer than I thought I needed to. No filters, no presets. Just patience and the willingness to stand still while the light does the work.
